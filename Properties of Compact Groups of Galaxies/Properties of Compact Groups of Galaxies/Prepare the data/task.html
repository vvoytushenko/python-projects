Theory

In this project, we will work with a dataset for astrophysics. It was designed for and by astrophysicists who study compact groups of galaxies (CGs), so it requires some explanation first. You will come across various terms, for example, the cosmological redshift, mean surface brightness of the intra-group light (IGL), galaxy low surface brightness (LSB) features, and so on. Don't worry, we will explain them to you as we go. Let's start!

  • The apparent magnitude \(m\) of a star or any other source is a number that tells how bright it seems to us at its distance from the Earth. The scale is logarithmic and inverse. A larger magnitude corresponds to a fainter source. In our case, the surface brightness is quoted on a magnitude scale, in magnitudes per square arcsecond. Usually, the surface brightness in magnitudes per square arcsecond is denoted by the Greek letter \(\mu\). For more information, refer to the great article Apparent versus Absolute Magnitude by Learn the Sky.
  • A redshift is an increase in the wavelength of electromagnetic radiation. Caused solely by the expansion of the Universe, the value of the cosmological redshift indicates the distance to the object. As the Universe expands, it stretches light that travels through space in a cosmological redshift phenomenon. The greater the redshift, the greater the distance the light has traveled. The Cosmological Redshift (YouTube) video by Jason Kendall can shed more light on this.
  • The vast majority of stars in galaxy groups are within their constituent galaxies. However, a small fraction of stars is expected to follow the global dark matter potential of the group. These stars form an intra-group light (IGL) component.
  • Within the frame of the Lambda cold dark matter (ΛCDM) cosmological model, galaxy interactions play a major role in the hierarchical galaxy formation theory. The hierarchical structure formation paradigm requires that every galaxy undergoes interactions throughout its lifetime. We know that each interaction leaves visible signatures around galaxies in faint shells, tidal tails, halos, plumes, and stellar streams. These low surface brightness (LSB) features around galaxies encode the hierarchical nature of the cosmological structure formation. Modern LSB methods of observation can detect these features. To get a more complete picture of this field of astronomy, you can study this review article: The Vera Rubin Observatory Legacy Survey of Space and Time and the Low Surface Brightness Universe. Description

The dataset contains information about CGs. The Group column displays the groups' names, z indicates redshifts, mean_mu states the IGL surface brightness, and the features column includes information regarding LSB features – 1 denotes the presence of LSB features for a CG, 0 means no such features. Take a look at the sample:

Objectives

Examine the box plot below for the mean surface brightness of the IGL in a group of compact groups of galaxies (CGs) with low surface brightness features and without them. Try to plot the same chart. Then find a mean redshift for these CGs.

As we've mentioned before, the magnitude system is inverse, so to place the brighter sources at the top, astronomers usually invert the magnitude axis.

To complete this stage:

  • Read the following dataset: groups.tsv. TSV stands for tab-separated values. Use the pandas.read_csv method and specify the delimiter parameter;
  • Remove the missing values from the dataset, you can use the dropna function;
  • Find a mean value for the IGL mean surface brightness in groups of galaxies with LSB features and without them;
  • Print two floating-point numbers (the mean value of the mean surface brightness for galaxies with LSB features and without them) split with a space.
